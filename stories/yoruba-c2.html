The Land Takes Shape

The Birth of the Land & the First Trees

Obatala stood upon the newborn earth, gazing at the vast stretch of land that had risen from the waters. The ground was firm beneath his feet, yet silent, waiting for the touch of life. Ile-Ife, the sacred city, had been formed—but the world was still empty.

He reached into the pouch tied around his waist and pulled forth the sacred palm nut given to him by Orunmila. Holding it in his hands, he whispered a prayer to Olodumare, then pressed it deep into the soil.

Moments later, the earth trembled softly, and the first tree began to rise. Its roots stretched outward, gripping the land, anchoring it in place. Its branches unfolded like arms reaching toward the sky.

Then, more trees followed—towering palms, ancient oaks, and lush fruit-bearing groves. Rivers formed, winding through the land, quenching the thirst of the new earth. Obatala smiled. The land was alive.

But creation was not yet complete. There was still one more task.

"The earth is strong, but it must have beings to walk upon it," Obatala murmured. "I must shape the first people."

And so, with divine purpose, he gathered the sacred clay.

The First Sculptures

The First Sculptures

Obatala knelt by the river’s edge, where the earth was soft and rich. He reached down, gathering handfuls of clay, feeling its weight in his palms. Carefully, he shaped the first human form—crafting arms, legs, and a face with deep, knowing eyes.

As he worked, he sang, a melody of creation carried on the wind:

"From the dust of the earth, I mold you,
From the breath of the sky, you shall rise."

One by one, he sculpted them, giving them strength and grace, making each unique. Yet, as he worked, the sun climbed higher, and the heat bore down upon him. His body longed for rest.

Nearby, the palm trees swayed, their fruit heavy with golden sap. The air carried the sweet scent of palm wine, fresh and inviting.

Obatala wiped the sweat from his brow. "A small drink will not harm my work," he thought.

He plucked a ripe fruit and drank. The wine was rich and warm, flowing through him like liquid gold. He took another sip… then another. His hands grew unsteady, his mind hazy.

Yet, in his drunken state, he continued sculpting. His hands, once precise, wavered. Some figures emerged with twisted limbs. Others had missing features or uneven shapes.

Still, Obatala worked, unaware of his mistake. The first humans had been made—but they were not all as he had intended.

The Imperfect Creation

The Imperfect Creation

As the night fell, Obatala looked upon his work. Dozens of figures stood before him, waiting for the breath of life. He raised his hands, calling upon Olodumare to awaken them.

A great wind swept through Ile-Ife, stirring the trees, whispering through the mountains. The figures trembled, then moved. Their chests rose with breath. Their eyes opened, seeing the world for the first time.

The first humans had been born.

Yet, as Obatala watched them walk, he realized his error. Some of them had bent backs, missing limbs, or other imperfections. His heart sank.

"What have I done?" he whispered.

Regret filled him, but he did not turn away. Instead, he stepped forward and spoke to them.

"You are my children, and I will care for you. Your form is sacred, for it is touched by the hands of a god."

And so, Obatala vowed to become the patron of those who were different. He would protect them, guide them, and remind the world that all beings, perfect or not, carried the divine spark of creation.

But the world was still young, and fate had more in store.

Would the other Orishas accept his work? And what of Olodumare—would he see this creation as a blessing or a mistake?
